Have you ever been tempted to spend big bucks on pricey skincare? 

If you have been intrigued by Crème de la Mer but were put off by its price tag, which ranges from $200 for one ounce to $570 for the 'value size (3.4 ounces),' there just might be a better, and much more budget-friendly, bet: Nivea Creme, which retails for $1.79 for a one-ounce tin. 

Toronto-based Regimen Lab is a skincare company that does beauty treatment reviews on TikTok and highlights the science behind everyone's favorite products, and recently, the brand explored the major similarities between both creams. 

The ingredients experts looked at everything included in both formulas in order to see how similar they really are.

The scientists investigation into the two creams was prompted by Irish DJ and content creator Annie Macmanus, 45, who shared a video in which she insisted 'Nivea is actually Crème de la Mer but in a different box,' while raving about the less expensive option because 'it keeps your skin really supple and moisturized all day.' 

'It's funny how a box and marketing can change your mind about something,' Annie mused, before asking her followers what they thought about the potential dupe. 

Regimen Lab then shared a response to the video and put Annie's theory to the test. 

'Is Nivea actually Crème de la Mer in a different box?! We tested it and… the results are very interesting!' the skincare account proclaimed. 

First, they took a look at both formulas side-by-side and found that Crème de la Mer, which is one of the world's most expensive skincare products, uses the same base as Nivea.

Nivea and Crème de la Mer share multiple ingredients, including mineral oil, petrolatum, glycerin, microcrystalline wax, lanolin alcohol, paraffin, magnesium sulfate, magnesium stearate decyl oleate, and panthenol.  

However, the main difference is in the fermented algae broth, which is the major ingredient in Crème de la Mer. 

'It also has some additional botanical oils, seed powders, and vitamins,' according to the scientists behind the video.

New York-based board-certified dermatologist Dr. Kristen Miller at Spectrum Skin and Laser explained that after 'taking a closer look at both ingredient lists, the majority of the ingredients are the same.' 

New York board-certified dermatologist Dr. Kristen Miller explained 'the majority of the ingredients are the same'

However, Dr. Miller clarified that 'a matching ingredient list does not necessarily translate into similar results.' 

'Regimen Lab performed two common methods of assessing moisturizer performance: a hydration and transepidermal water loss (TEWL) reduction test,' Dr. Miller said, adding they 'ultimately showed largely similar results.' 

'The tests concluded that they are comparable in these two common measurements but this does not completely assess their performance as a moisturizer,' according to Dr. Miller. 

Dr. Miller believes the biggest difference comes down to La Mer's 'infamous proprietary ingredient,' which is 'a mix of seaweed - primarily a species of giant kelp - that goes through a fermentation process, along with added botanical oils, seed powders and vitamins that Nivea does not' have.

'How this affects other aspects of skin appearance besides hydration and moisturizing is harder to quantify,' Dr. Miller said. 

So while they might be hydrating and contain similar properties, Dr. Miller concluded that 'ultimately these are not the same product.'
