De La Rue: UK's Banknote Printer Suspends Operations in Kenya

 Posted on March 26, 2024  |  3 minutes  |  428 words  |  Aldo Pusey
  • De La Rue has shut down its printing operations in Kenya citing low market demand in the country
  • According to the UK's banknote printer, there has been a reduced demand in the country, meaning for the next 12 months, they won't be printing any banknotes
  • This comes days after the printing company was implicated in a tax scandal amounting to KSh 1.1 billion

British banknote printer De La Rue PLC has shut down its operations in Kenya citing low market demand.

De La Rue bows out of Kenya

According to Reuters, De La Rue PLC on Friday assured investors of business opportunities in Kenya and elsewhere, after suspending its printing operations based on reduced offers.

De La Rue is a Basingstoke, England-based company which designs and produces banknotes, secure polymer substrate, and banknote security features for central banks and currency-issuing authorities.

This means that for the next twelve months, the famous printing company will not be printing any bank notes.

De La Rue had been operating in the country through a joint venture with the government of Kenya, in which the Nairobi government holds a 40% equity share.

The company noted that the joint venture remains active, and said it was exploring business opportunities with a view to restarting production "if the economic climate permits".

De La Rue continues to operate with three banknote sites in the UK, Malta and Sri Lanka. This is a reduction from five sites operating in 2020.

This comes days after the High Court in Nairobi dismissed an appeal from one of De La Rue's subsidiaries related to a historical tax issue, equating to KSh 1.1 billion.

The printing company noted that it was disappointed with the ruling, and added that its subsidiary was preparing a further appeal.

Economic expert advises Ruto to print more money

In a different business report, progressive economist Tony Mwiti commended President William Ruto for his bottom-up approach but asked him to help Kenyans bare the recession burden.

He likened the current situation to the US's way of dealing with recession while urging the commander-in-chief to carefully print more money to sustain the economy.

"Your model is brilliant, but if you want to afford its ideals without the catch-22 situation of either burdening citizens with new taxes or borrowing new loans kindly consider Deficit Funding," he said.
