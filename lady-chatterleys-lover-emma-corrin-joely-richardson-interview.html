Emma Corrin &amp; Joely Richardson interview

 Posted on February 1, 2024  |  5 minutes  |  1022 words  |  Reinaldo Massengill

"I’ve never been more scared in my life," Emma Corrin says, their piercing blue eyes wide. "Or… more cold." The actor is describing one of the most magnificent and memorable scenes in Netflix’s luminous new film adaption of DH Lawrence’s transgressive 1932 novel Lady Chatterley’s Lover, in which Corrin plays the titular character. Following WWI, a young, spirited Constance Chatterley finds herself stifled by her soured, sexless marriage to Clifford, whose war injuries have left him paralysed from the waist down; eventually discovering a profound emotional, physical and intellectual connection with the estate gamekeeper, Mellors (who, in this version, reads James Joyce and is portrayed by Jack O’Connell).

Emma Corrin and Matthew Duckett as Lady and Lord Chatterley

In the scene Corrin is describing, Constance and Mellors are leaping, naked, around a thistly meadow, in a downpour, with apparent wild abandon, exulting in their bodies and nature with an almost Garden-of-Eden-esque assurance. Evidently, the duo’s cavalier performance belies their actual experience of filming it, in a Welsh valley last year.

"It was wild," remembers the actor, who rose to fame as a note-perfect young Princess Diana in series 4 of The Crown. "We wanted a sense of spontaneity, so we didn’t rehearse it; there were six rain machines going and… they just shouted action. I went first, then Jack ran out and we were running around like hooligans, completely starkers. I remember us looking into each other’s eyes, seeing the mutual terror and that look of 'What are we doing?!' that then said, let’s just go for it. It was like a primal liberation."

Corrin with Jack O’Connell as Constance Chatterley and Oliver Mellors

The film also stars Joely Richardson, in a poignantly full-circle moment for the much-loved British actress: she played Lady Chatterley in the BBC’s classic 1993 series, opposite Sean Bean (and they, too, enacted the aforementioned rain dance, but with classical music played full volume on set, rather than the latest version’s African drums). Nearly 30 years later, she has stepped into the shoes of Mrs Bolton, the wise, world-worn woman hired to look after Lord Chatterley, thus freeing Connie to find herself, and happiness, elsewhere.

Netflix

Joely Richardson as Mrs Bolton, Emma Corrin as Lady Constance and Faye Marsay as Hilda

"I was excited to see a new take on Connie," Richardson says now, glancing at her co-star with a kind, and rather conspiratorial, smile. "Your Lady Chatterley is utterly modern, Emma – which you are yourself, in the best sense – you’re an activist, you’re redefining culture – and to have that represented in this lead character is wonderful. I’m so pleased we’re bringing it to a whole new generation."

Your Lady Chatterley is utterly modern, Emma

"It was like the handing on of a baton," Corrin says, of taking up the reins of this legendary literary role from Richardson. Decades apart, they both were drawn to Lady Chatterley’s strength and ebullience in a stuffy, patriarchal society. "Quite often in films, especially period dramas, women are confronted with being dissatisfied with their relationship or their life, and they don’t do anything about it," Corrin continues. "But Connie isn’t afraid to fight for what she wants and needs – and for who she loves."

Netflix

Lady Chatterley outside her marital home, Wragby Hall

As for portraying that famously physical love on screen, the pair had different, but similarly positive experiences. Intimacy coordinators were not part of the industry landscape 30 years ago, and Richardson welcomes their presence on set today. "It’s dance and movement in those scenes, so they need choreographing. I completely freaked out before my first ever sex scene – they’re so raw," she says, laughing.

"It’s true – it’s so vulnerable," Corrin nods. Both agree that stepping into character is, helpfully, akin to putting on a "cloak of protection’", because it’s not "you, yourself" in the scene. Humour helps too – and in the case of filming intimate moments in period dramas, amusement often arises when dealing with historical lingerie. "I had one ridiculous scene with Sean up against a tree, and as per the book, he pulls off Connie’s underwear," Richardson remembers. "But they just went on and on and on, like a magician’s trick. He was like ‘God almighty, how long are your knickers?!"

"Totally," agrees Corrin. "I was lucky to have the same with Jack – we share a sense of humour, and were both on the same page about the sex scenes always needing to have purpose – the result is that the changing intimacy of those scenes charts Connie and Mellors’ emotional evolution together."

I hope it inspires conversations about female pleasure

In the new version, Corrin and Richardson both particularly love the line in which Lady Chatterley tells Mellors that what he has, is tenderness. "Everyone always goes, ‘oh, Lady Chatterley’s so sexual’ but actually no, it’s about intimacy, human connection and nature," Richardson says. "DH Lawrence very much felt the story’s themes were reflecting the pagan."

Courtesy

And while the relationship between Mellors and Lady Chatterley is the central plot, there are some terrific female characters surrounding it, who contribute nuance to the film. Firstly, Connie’s sister: "Hilda is so great," Corrin grins, of the fabulously straight-talking sibling played by Faye Marsay. "I’m obsessed with her. And I think Connie has a really special friendship with Mrs Bolton." Richardson agrees, explaining that perhaps the older woman "wants other people to have the chance at the love she has experienced, having loved her own husband so much".

Netflix

Richardson and Corrin on set

Without giving too much away, the movie concludes on an uplifting note. "The fact it’s a happy ending surprises lots of people, which says so much about our expectations – that we don’t really believe love can exist for the sake of love," Corrin says. "I want people to finish the film with a sense of hope – and I hope it inspires conversations about female pleasure and how it can be something doesn’t need to raise eyebrows. It can just exist and be celebrated."
