Eva Longoria Talks Casa del Sol and Upcoming Directorial Debuts

 Posted on March 1, 2024  |  3 minutes  |  623 words  |  Jenniffer Sheldon

Eva Longoria is a bit of a Latinx icon, and she's not afraid of leaning into it.

The actress-turned-activist—and now entrepreneur and director—has remained a Hollywood fixture since the early aughts, when she starred in the hit ABC series Desperate Housewives. But Longoria has evolved past the limitations that Hollywood often places upon its Latina talents. For the last few years, she's been stretching her production and business strengths, including partnering with Casa del Sol to launch a female-forward approach to tequila.

"I've been approached for the last 20 years to enter the tequila space simply because of who I am—because I'm Mexican American, because it's very authentic to my culture and my heritage—but every time I was approached, it was a celebrity grab. It wasn't the right partners, and it just wasn't the right reason," Longoria tells BAZAAR.com at her New York Fashion Week kickoff dinner hosted with L'Agence in New York City last week. "Casa del Sol was the first company that came wanting to honor Mexican heritage first and honor the art of making tequila."

Longoria stresses that she's trying to get the tequila game right. Casa del Sol doesn't consider itself a "tequila for women" so much as a brand that places women in key positions—the kind that are often occupied by men. The brand makes a point of employing mothers and other caretakers in the region, who help craft the leather detailing on each bottle, and providing crucial dental care for the agaveros who harvest the agave plants that go into the actual spirit.

"We have a female president, our distillery is female ran, we have a female VP of operations. And there's so many brands, right? But tequila is the number one spirit consumed by women. So why shouldn't women participate in making it?" Longoria says. "And it's not about going into the region and exploiting the culture. Investing in the community was really important, and it's only gonna get bigger as our brand grows. We're going to continue to be there in an authentic way, in an authentic space."

The actress has dedicated her career to changing the narrative surrounding Latinos in America. She's been living in Mexico City part time as she gears up to host CNN+'s Searching for Mexico and will make her directorial debut with not one but two separate films this year. The first, a documentary called La Guerra Civil, looks back at the 1996 boxing match between Oscar de la Hoya and Julio César Chávez, which challenged the antiquated way society decides who gets to call themselves Mexican. The second, Flamin' Hot, is a scripted feature following the true story of Mexican-American Richard Montañez, the man who invented the Flamin' Hot Cheeto—which swiftly became a quintessential snack within the Latino and Black communities, and a global phenomenon.

"[With Searching for Mexico,] there's going to be a huge educational aspect to showing people how beautiful the culture really is and how it's truly embedded into our lives. … The same people who say, 'Yay, Taco Tuesday,' are the same people saying, 'Build that wall,'" Longoria explains. "And then, La Guerra Civil is about so much more than just boxing, which is what I wanted it to be. And Flamin' Hot—oh, my God, it's going to make you cry 10 times. It has a similar feel and tone to that of King Richard and The Pursuit of Happyness. … We're still in post, but it's going to be amazing."
